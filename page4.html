FrootBeer Impact Statement

The FrootBeer Craft Cidery came about in answer to a personal need, the ability to create a craft beverage that wasn’t beer, but could offer the same diversity of flavors, aromas and textures as craft beer. Craft beverages derived from fruit accomplish this and result in a gluten-free product that those who are intolerant can enjoy freely. With only 775 cideries and meaderies in the U.S. as of 2018 (West, 2018), cideries promise much more potential for continued market growth compared to craft beer breweries of which there were 7,698 that same year (Brewers Association, 2020). The expectation of FrootBeer is that its ciders could easily translate into a flourishing business, and that our creative use of craft beer brewing techniques will bring about a variety of ciders that impact the craft brewing industry in a big way.

My introduction to hard Cider, cider that had been fermented to create an alcoholic beverage, was in Germany in 1990. My neighbor/landlord was a farmer who annually gathered all of the apples from the trees on his farmland, crushed and pressed them, storing the juice in a large barrel and allowing nature to create apfelmost, his farmhouse beverage. It was not for the faint of heart, it was bitter and sour, with an alcohol content that stood up to any German beer. His way of making cider harkens to how it had been done naturally for over 3000 years, with evidence of cider production dating back to ancient Egypt (O’Malley, 2020).

In the U.S. we have Prohibition to thank for the disruption of cider-making, and the cheap proliferation of the American beer industry after Prohibition for its failure to rebound (Rupp, 2015). Today however, ciders are making a comeback. You can see them on liquor store, convenience mart and supermarket shelves alongside beer and other fermented beverages. For the most part, these are sweet apple beverages with few characteristics to distinguish one from another. Why does that have to be the end for cider? Thanks to the craft beer industry, brewing technology and the creative use of yeasts, hops and other natural ingredients have all come together to create a bountiful variety of beverages all created from wheat, rye and barley. Using the same techniques as the craft beer industry, FrootBeer and a growing number of other cideries, with apple, pear and countless other fruit juices liberate an even greater variety of flavors, textures and aromas than craft beer can.

Looking ahead, expect growth from this nascent cider movement. Today’s generation is constantly seeking out the new, the innovate, something theirs. As the current cultural desire for craft beer wanes, they will seek out other options that promise an equal, or better quality of refreshment. Enter ciders, created naturally, from natural ingredients and fostering the generation and growth of orchards, gardens, and bees that produce those ingredients. They offer much for the drinker, the entrepreneur, agriculture and the environment. Given this generation’s desire for social and environmental impact, the potential for development of cider brewing into an industry in its own right is certainly plausible if not probable.

I can imagine, generations in the future, craft cideries holding an equal market share with craft breweries in the sale of fermented beverages. With that will have come similar changes to the restaurant, yeast, hop, agriculture and beverage industries, as those generated by the proliferation of craft breweries. Cider themed restaurants and pubs, prolific cideries and taphouses featuring cider styles, the development of special yeast strains and hops for ciders, increased emphasis on orchard horticulture and cider distribution will all be features of this future. As will perhaps, on a small scale, the renewal and management of lost arboreal lands. An outlook I’d like to think future generations could get behind.

The future has already started. Sure, pull a cider from the supermarket shelf, better yet try one at your local taphouse. Or best, seek out a real cidery near you and tell them what you like: crisp and light, tart and fruity, bittersweet, or dark and heavy, pick your preferred descriptors. They’ll have something for you, and you’ll find incredible flavor, be supporting a business that is local, and the future I envision.
